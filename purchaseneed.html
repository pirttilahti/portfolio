Feature design

Shifting purchasing from an art to a maintainable process

(V1 2022-2023)



IN SHORT

I led the design of a new approach to material requirement planning and purchase tracking in an ERP system, transforming purchasing from a craft reliant on individual expertise into a maintainable and repeatable process. The solution allows users to view everything related to purchase need from one view instead of navigating from view to view to compare data.

Challenge

When you think about buying, it doesn‚Äôt feel like something that is hard to do. All of us buy things, all the time. Purchasing or procurement as a profession, that is another story.

Many might not realize, but manufacturing and wholesale companies usually need a separate role for purchasing. This role might focus solely on procurement. Why? Well knowing where to buy, what to buy, when to buy and how much can be bought or what is an optimal amount to buy, involves A LOT of work. Especially when it comes to solutions that are available widely.

As part of our research we noticed an opportunity, material need at different time intervals is really hard to manage.



Research

We originally set out to conduct generative research to understand production and production planning. Along the way, we uncovered an unexpected opportunity.

When companies manufacture, they also need to buy materials. When you manufacture a chair, you need wood, glue, screws, and varnish. If you don‚Äôt have something, you won‚Äôt be finishing the product. Imagine building 50 types of chairs with unique types of wood and screws... You‚Äôre probably starting to get the problem. At this point we only knew that they need materials and that is what others solution providers are doing, listing the amounts needed.



Mapping the experience

By thematizing the interview data we saw the unexpected theme arise, "acquisitions". We found that purchasing plays a far more central role than we had anticipated. It's one of the most critical parts of company operations.

After user interviews and factory visits, we didn‚Äôt have it all laid out and ready to go, but through understanding the whole flow of production and materials, we were able to iterate on the original idea.

I dove deep into what happens and when and what needs to be known at which stage. Mapping the experience along the way to finally arrive with an idea.

Early prototype

Through multiple mentions of timing the purchase, I created a very rough sketch of a solution idea where we showed the material need for the current week. We took it to customers to help us pin down the real problem.

During testing, one of our customers mentioned a key insight:

‚ÄúWe need to know 3 months in advance what materials we need. If our materials aren‚Äôt there at that time, we aren‚Äôt making the product‚Äù

The key was to understand that a week is nothing. Very few factories can suddenly get products delivered for friday if they realize the need on wednesday, thursday, or even monday. Most will have to order it at least a week before. We were able to take this insight and turn it into one of the main differentiating functionalities in our product.

Second prototype

The sketch was iterated and the idea of showing days was switched to weeks by default. This small change, changed the game.

Now we had a solution that changed the game. Planning is what acquisition management is about. Well at least after the sourcing is done. What was left, was user testing.

As always, there were usability issues found during the second usability testing round, but they were minor. 100% of the users understood the benefit of the solution and many found it a novel approach.

To this day I don‚Äôt know of any other product that has an approach that could compete.


Outcome and learnings

Customers got a novel way of approaching purchase need, changing the situation from needing someone who is always up-to-date on each item to simply telling users what to focus on. It also highlighted what else needs to be done to improve the overall product.
